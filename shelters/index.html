War, Civilian and Home

Throughout history, wars have always had negative influences on human society. The brutality of war is often linked with casualties of soldiers on the battlefield. However, civilians are equally affected during wartime. On this webpage, the impact of war on civilians and their homes will be discussed.

Impact of war on civilians

Civilians are affected by war in many ways, with the most significant one being physical harm.
In recent centuries, with the advance in technology and change in warfare ideology, civilian fatality has increased noticeably. From 5% around the beginning of the 19th century, to 15% during World War I, to 65% by the end of World War II and to over 90% in wars during 1990's. More and more civilians are losing their lives as a result of the conflicts.[1]

The film Grave of the Fireflies embodied this with the scenes of civilians escaping from incendiary bombs (8:20), civilian casualties placed in school acting as hospital(15:40) and cremation of dead civilians(19:50)

War also brings psychological trauma to civilians.
A survey aimed to study mentality and disability in postwar Afghanistan revealed that 67.7% of respondents show symptoms of depression, 71.7% of them show symptoms of anxiety.[2]
War leads to lack of supply.
"Among the 815 million people suffering from chronic malnutrition in 2016, 60 percent lived in areas affected by armed conflict." [3]
This is also seen in the film Grave of the Fireflies, where rationing in Japan during World War II is presented, as well as food shortage for many civilians. At 59:30, we can see the line of a farmer: "I'm a farmer, but even I don't have any rice to share."

Civilian displacement caused by war

Many civilians affected either directly (injury, casualty) or indirectly (destruction of infrastructure, lack of supply) by war would settle in regions with safer conditions.

At the end of 2022, around 108.4 million people are forced to displace worldwide, with 35.3 million of them being refugees, 62.5 million of them being IDPS (internally displaced person(s)). Among these refugees, 6 million of them returned to where they're from.
76% of these people are hosted in low- and middle-income countries, with 20% of them hosted in the least developed countries.[4]
22% of these refugees - about 6.6 million people live in refugee camps[5], while the majority of them (over 60% of refugees, around 50% of IDPs) live in urban areas.[6]

People in refugee camps receive humanitarian aids including but not limited to shelter, medical care, food and water.[7]However, these people face many difficulties. Such as lack of education and suboptimal living environment. Due to the number of children exceeding the number of students that schools can accommodate in refugee camps, about 48% of refugee children are unable to attend school.[8]Many Syrian families living in refugee camps don't have roofs over their shelter.[9]In a report about a refugee camp in Palestine, it is revealed that 48% of houses had one window only, and 50% of them had two windows, which leads to poor ventilation. Furthermore, concrete roofs are commonly found in houses, which causes presence of damp.[10]According to another report about a refugee camp in Lebanon, 83.3% of households indicated cockroach infestation.[11]

This can be linked to the podcast What Shelter Means to Me, in which houseless artist Denis Elmore described his housing environment: "And this mold in every place, ceiling is wide open, the bathroom is ... a little cut-out with a toilet space" (9:30)

Generally speaking, life is easier for refugees living in urban area compared to those living in refugee camps. Although they are still met with numerous challenges. Take the case of Hindu Rohingya refugees who migrated to Bangladesh as example, it's much harder for them to find quality jobs compared to the locals due to racial discrimination.[12]
Many Ukranian refugees only have limited access to basic services. Many don't have free health care due to the lack of awareness towards refugees' rights. Only 30% - 70% of child refugees are enrolled in local schools due to language barrier and local school capacity.[13]

Meaning of home to war-affected civilians

The concept of home is the concept of appropriation of a space which means the aspect of making something your own by making it feel like your own.

There's the main aspect that makes home feel like home. Number 1 is material aspects which contain protection, comfort and presentation of aesthetic values. Number 2 is the spatial aspect which contains zoning, housing typology, and connectedness to the surrounding landscape. Number 3 is the relational aspect which contains relationships with other people and the sense of attachment and security. The fourth aspect to define home is the temporal aspect which is the memories of lost homes or the images of an ideal home.

All this is very difficult for a war affected civilian to do, if you look at the war in Ukraine, they're not capable of doing aspects to create a sense of home because of the war. They are not capable of doing it either because it is destroyed and will never look as beautiful as it once did or because they have lost someone who made it feel like home.

There is also another aspect for why they will probably never feel at home in their country. They have seen terrible things be done or they have had to do terrible things to be able to survive and it is very difficult to feel at home. Home does not necessarily mean a building of some sorts, it could mean someone. In the book home at page 117 in the section beyond home speaks of the fact that home can sometime be beyond just a building but a person, but this could be hard for a war-affected civilian because of the fact that you could have lost the person that was you're home ether because you lost touch with them either because they have died. So it is much more difficult for a war affected civilian to make a home connection with a human being because after going through a war and surviving it could be difficult to trust someone new to that level.
